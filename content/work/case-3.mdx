<Chapter title="Background">

Checkout is where trust is either earned or lost. For a direct-to-consumer brand doing most of its business on mobile, a checkout flow that was ported from desktop without rethinking the interaction model was quietly hemorrhaging revenue.

The engagement was a focused 10-week sprint: audit the existing flow, identify the highest-impact friction points, redesign and rebuild with A/B testing infrastructure in place from day one.

</Chapter>

<Chapter title="What Broke">

A session recording analysis of 2,400 checkout sessions revealed a consistent pattern: users who reached the payment step completed at 61%. Users who abandoned at payment cited two issues in follow-up surveys: "didn't feel secure" and "too many steps."

The existing flow had seven screens between cart and confirmation. The address form was a ported desktop layout — four fields in a row, thumb-unfriendly — and the payment step loaded a third-party iframe that had a noticeably different visual style than the rest of the brand.

Lighthouse on a mid-tier Android: **LCP 4.1s on the checkout page.** The page was shipping 340KB of unoptimized JavaScript, including a payment library that could have been deferred until the user reached that step.

</Chapter>

<Chapter title="Approach">

The redesign had two tracks running in parallel:

**Track 1 — Performance.** Code-split the payment library behind a dynamic import. Lazy-load non-critical assets below the fold. Move address validation from client-side regex to a lightweight edge function (faster feedback, less JS shipped). Result: LCP dropped to **1.7s**.

**Track 2 — UX.** Collapsed seven screens to four using progressive disclosure. The address form was rebuilt as a single-column stacked layout optimized for thumb reach zones. Stripe Elements was re-themed to match brand typography and color — the iframe content now looks like it belongs.

Added a persistent progress indicator (step 1 of 4) so users always know where they are in the flow. Added autofill-friendly `autocomplete` attributes on every field (obvious in retrospect, but absent from the original).

An A/B test ran for three weeks with a 50/50 split.

</Chapter>

<Chapter title="Results">

The test reached statistical significance (95%) in week two.

- **Mobile conversion rate:** +28% (variant vs. control)
- **Cart abandonment at payment step:** −51%
- **Checkout LCP (mobile):** 4.1s → 1.7s
- **Average checkout duration:** 4.2 min → 2.1 min
- **Support tickets: "payment not working"** (mostly user frustration, not actual errors): −63%

The variant was fully shipped at end of week three. Annualized revenue impact based on conversion lift: significant enough to fully fund a second engineering sprint on the account page.

</Chapter>

<Chapter title="Takeaways">

Checkout UX is part perception, part performance. The security perception gap — users who said it "didn't feel secure" — wasn't about the actual security of the implementation. It was about the visual mismatch between the brand page and the payment iframe. Solving that was mostly a theming exercise, but it moved conversion.

The biggest performance win was the deferred payment library import. The library was ~140KB of JavaScript that every user was forced to download before they'd even seen the address form. Dynamic importing it dropped that weight from the initial load completely.

Invest in autocomplete. It's the easiest checkout improvement no one does.

</Chapter>
